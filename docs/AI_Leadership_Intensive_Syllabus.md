## Program Description

While technical AI bootcamps teach tools, leadership remains the critical bottleneck to successful AI implementation. Participants in this program need more than prompt engineering; they need the strategic frameworks to translate AI capabilities into business value, manage organizational change, and lead ethically in an AI-driven economy.

Based on the curriculum taught at UC Berkeley, this intensive moves beyond the “how-to” of AI tools to focus on the “why,” “when,” and “how to lead” of AI strategy. The program treats human-AI collaboration as the new core leadership competency, working alongside AI tools throughout the program to think, frame, decide, and lead.

Over two weeks, teams scope a real problem and ship an AI-powered app that does one thing well, for example, a social-media content generator, a study tool, or a simple tracker. In Week 1, they turn that idea into a Product Requirement Document that doubles as a build spec; in Week 2, they build, deploy, stress-test the responsible-AI questions against their real product, and present it on Demo Day.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of the program, participants will be able to:

- **Build and ship:** use AI-augmented tools to build and deploy a working application, with no prior coding experience required.
- **Strategic AI implementation:** apply proven leadership frameworks to AI project management and team dynamics.
- **Technical-business translation:** bridge AI capabilities and business requirements by scoping and specifying a product.
- **Human-AI collaboration:** design where the human stays in the loop, what the AI explains, and how trust is built or broken.
- **Responsible leadership:** evaluate a real product against established responsible-AI principles before it ships.

## Assessment & Grading

Assessment combines the quality of what you ship, peer collaboration, and demonstration of strategic thinking. Grades are distributed as follows:

| Component | Weight |
|---|---:|
| Class Participation & Peer Collaboration (Individual) | 20% |
| PRD / Build Spec — Week 1 Deliverable (Team) | 30% |
| Final Project + Demo Day — deployed app + repo (Team) | 50% |

### Class Participation & Peer Collaboration (20%)

Active, meaningful contribution to discussions, build sessions, and peer reviews. Participation is about engagement and effort, not eloquence. Contributing through building, asking questions, and supporting teammates all count. Quality of contribution matters more than quantity.

### PRD / Build Spec — Week 1 (30%)

Your team's Product Requirement Document for the app: the problem, who it is for, the one core thing it does, the role AI plays, the role humans play, scope, and a clear signal for what “working” looks like. Developed through advanced prompting and finalized in the Day 5 workshop after structured peer review. It doubles as the build specification for Week 2.

### Final Project + Demo Day (50%)

The centerpiece. Success means your team ships a deployed, working app that does the one core thing you set out to build, and presents it on Demo Day. The bar is “it works, and a stranger can use it,” not “it's impressive.” Polish, extra features, and ambition are what move a good grade to a great one. The deliverable is the deployed app, a repository with a clear README that shows the team's human-AI collaboration, and a Demo Day presentation (with portfolio materials) assessed on clarity and demonstration of what you built and learned.

## Program Policies

### Attendance & Independent Work

Because this is a compressed, build-focused intensive, attendance at every session is expected; each day builds on the previous one and on your teammates' work. Attendance is recorded at the start of each session, and more than one absence may affect your Class Participation grade. Beyond scheduled hours, teams are expected to put in meaningful independent build time — shipping a working product in two weeks depends on it. If you have a disability-related absence accommodation, you are still required to attend and participate to receive full credit; inform the instructor as early as possible.

### Late Work

Unless otherwise stated, there is no credit for late assignments. Team deliverables in particular cannot be late, because they feed the next day's work and other teams' feedback.

### Use of AI

This program's relationship to AI use is explicit and central. Participants are expected to use AI tools throughout the build; doing so is part of the pedagogy.

- **Disclose your AI collaboration.** Commit messages, pull-request descriptions, and the repo README should document AI-augmented work honestly.
- **Original thinking remains your responsibility.** AI may help you draft, debug, design, and build; final decisions, framing, and reflection must be your own.
- **Cite human sources properly.** Copying text or ideas from another human source without attribution is plagiarism and will result in a failing grade on the work and possible further disciplinary action.

If you are uncertain whether your use of AI crosses a line, ask. The default posture in this program is transparency.

### Building Responsibly

Once you start wiring up real services, a few ground rules apply. Never commit API keys, passwords, or other secrets to a public repository. Use environment variables and keep credentials out of your code. Respect the terms of service, rate limits, and data-use policies of any API or data source you connect to, and do not collect personal data you do not need. The app you ship is yours; build it as something you would be comfortable putting your name on.

## Week 1 — Foundation & Application

## Day 1 — AI Evolution & Strategic Frameworks

**Monday, July 6 · 10:00 AM – 2:00 PM**

### Session Overview

We open not with the deep history of AI but with the much shorter, more relevant history of the current moment: the period since ChatGPT's launch in November 2022. In roughly three years the field has moved from “an interesting chatbot” to foundation models, agentic systems, and AI-augmented tools that let non-coders ship working products. We map the inflection points and locate ourselves on that map.

We then establish the program's thesis: human-AI collaboration is a learnable competency, and leadership — not tooling — is the real bottleneck. Participants form teams of approximately five, identify a first project-management lead (“Discover & Define”), and orient toward the central question: what does it mean to lead, build, and work alongside AI as a collaborator rather than a tool?

### Learning Objectives

- Locate the current state of AI within the brief history since ChatGPT (Nov 2022).
- Articulate the program thesis: human-AI collaboration as a core leadership competency.
- Form a team and identify the first project-management lead.
- Understand the build arc: scope an app, ship it, present it on Demo Day.

### Required Readings

- None assigned for this session.

### Discussion / Cold-Call Questions

- What was your first encounter with a generative AI tool, and how did it change what you thought was possible?
- Name one moment since November 2022 that you'd point to as a turning point. Why?
- The thesis is that human-AI collaboration is a learnable skill, not a personality trait. Do you agree? What would evidence look like?
- If you had to ship a working app in two weeks using AI tools, what would you not yet know how to do?

### Assignments & Deliverables

- Before Day 2: Confirm access to a modern LLM.

## Day 2 — Hands-On Prompting as a Strategic Skill

**Tuesday, July 7 · 10:00 AM – 2:00 PM**

### Session Overview

Participants work hands-on with LLMs, treating prompting as a leadership skill: precision, context-setting, iterative refinement, and knowing when to push back on the model. We run side-by-side comparisons between models, examine where LLMs reliably fail, and study the structure of effective prompts in strategic, analytical, and creative contexts.

A guest builder or founder joins the second half of class to demonstrate how AI tools show up in real product work — so participants see “this is possible” before they touch a keyboard on Day 3.

### Learning Objectives

- Develop a working mental model of what current LLMs do well and badly.
- Practice structured prompt design (context, examples, constraints, iteration).
- Compare LLM outputs across two or more models on the same task.
- Begin documenting human-AI collaboration in a structured way.

### Discussion / Cold-Call Questions

- Where did the LLM surprise you, positively or negatively, during the exercise?
- What makes one prompt produce a useful output and a similar prompt a useless one?
- When should a leader trust an AI output? When should they not?
- What does the model not know about your problem that you would need to tell it?

### Assignments & Deliverables

- “First Contact” artifact: a meaningful output produced in a focused 20-prompt session, with prompts and outputs documented. The first item in your portfolio.
- Before Day 3: Install your chosen build tool (Replit, Claude Code, or Cursor) per the setup guide.

## Day 3 — Vibe Coding 101 — Your First Build

**Wednesday, July 8 · 10:00 AM – 2:00 PM**

### Session Overview

The first building session. Live demonstration: an instructor builds a working small app from scratch using an AI-augmented tool in roughly 30 minutes, narrating the human-AI collaboration as it happens. Students then pair-build a small personal tool of their own — a timer, a habit tracker, a recipe randomizer, anything. Students with stronger technical backgrounds pair with students who have less.

We cover the minimum-viable workflow needed for the rest of the program — clone, commit, push, pull request — and the basics of deployment, so every team gets a “Hello World” live on the public internet. Just enough to collaborate and ship, no more.

### Learning Objectives

- Use an AI-augmented tool to build and run a working application, no prior coding required.
- Understand the basic workflow: clone, commit, push, branch, pull request.
- Recognize the patterns of effective vibe-coding (clear intent, iterative correction, knowing when to stop).
- Push a first repository and get a “Hello World” deployment live.

### Required Readings

- Willison, S. (2025). *Not all AI-assisted programming is vibe coding*.

### Discussion / Cold-Call Questions

- Describe one moment today when the AI produced something quickly that you then had to stop and fix. What told you something was off?
- When you overrode the AI and did it your own way, what did you know that it didn't?
- Where would you trust vibe coding to move you fast, and where would you slow down and stay in control?
- Willison separates accepting code you don't read from understanding everything that ships. Which were you doing today, and which should you do for your real project?

### Assignments & Deliverables

- By end of class: every student pushes a repo containing a working small app, and each team has a “Hello World” deployed URL.

## Day 4 — Scoping Your App & the PRD as Build Spec

**Thursday, July 9 · 10:00 AM – 2:00 PM**

### Session Overview

Each team turns a problem worth solving into a narrowly scoped, buildable app. Possible projects include: a social-media content generator, a study tool, a simple tracker, or anything the team is genuinely curious about. Using advanced prompting, teams draft a Product Requirement Document (PRD) that doubles as a build spec: the one core thing the app does, who it is for, where AI fits and where humans stay in control, and a simple signal for what “working” looks like.

AI projects fail in distinct ways, from scope creep enabled by tooling that “could do anything” to the deceptive ease of a demo that doesn't hold up with real users. We cover lightweight planning suited to a two-week build (drawing on Nenni et al.) and the discipline to cut features that aren't essential. Teams get a first version of their actual app deployed before the workshop.

### Learning Objectives

- Scope an app down to one core thing that can realistically ship in two weeks.
- Draft a PRD/build spec: problem, user, the one core thing, AI's role, human's role, success signal.
- Use advanced prompting to critique and sharpen your own draft.
- Name one feature you'd cut, and the condition that would tell you to cut it.

### Required Readings

- Nenni et al. (2025) — introduction and framework/findings sections.

### Discussion / Cold-Call Questions

- What's a feature your team is tempted to build “because the AI makes it easy”? Should you?
- What single signal would tell you whether your app is actually working?
- What's the most boring possible version of your app? Is it actually worse than the ambitious one?
- What does the model not know about your users that you would have to supply?

### Assignments & Deliverables

- Toward the PRD (team): a complete PRD/build-spec draft plus a first deployed version of the actual app, ready for peer review on Day 5.

## Day 5 — PRD Workshop & Peer Review

**Friday, July 10 · 10:00 AM – 2:00 PM**

### Session Overview

The Week 1 deliverable comes together. Rather than present to the full room, teams review in small clusters: the class splits into groups of three to four teams, and within each cluster every team pitches its PRD and demos whatever is deployed, however rough, while the instructor circulates. Feedback focuses on scope realism (can you actually ship this in the time left?) and the human-AI collaboration design (where does AI fit, where does it not, and why?).

Teams revise on the spot and finalize the PRD/build spec. End of class includes the project-management handoff: the outgoing “Discover & Define” lead hands context and open questions to the incoming “Build & Ship” lead, who takes over in Week 2.

### Learning Objectives

- Pitch a PRD that is realistic in scope and explicit about its human-AI collaboration design.
- Demo work in progress to a friendly audience and absorb feedback constructively.
- Finalize a professional PRD/build spec to drive Week 2.
- Hand off leadership in a way that preserves context for a teammate.

### Required Readings

- Nenni et al. (2025) — framework/findings sections (carry-over).

### Discussion / Cold-Call Questions

- What did another team's PRD or demo make you reconsider about your own?
- What's the one question you'd want to ask a user before building any further?
- PMs handing off: what's the single most important thing your successor needs to know?

### Assignments & Deliverables

- PRD / Build Spec (team — Week 1 deliverable, 30%): final PRD covering problem, user, the one core thing, AI's role, human's role, scope, and success signal, plus the PM handoff notes.

## Day 6 — Leading AI-Augmented Teams + Build Kickoff

**Monday, July 13 · 1:30 – 5:00 PM**

### Session Overview

Week 2 shifts into building. We anchor the day in Edmondson's work on psychological safety, applied to the specific challenge of leading a team where members bring very different technical fluency — and where, with AI tools, people who can't code can now contribute meaningfully. The incoming “Build & Ship” PM takes formal leadership of each team, and teams begin building in earnest with the instructor circulating.

- Whose voice gets quieter when AI can do the technical work? How do you protect against that?

## Day 7 — Designing the Human-AI Interaction

**Tuesday, July 14 · 1:30 – 4:00 PM**

### Session Overview

A focused session on the experience of the people who will actually use your app. Brown's design-thinking lens provides the frame; Microsoft's Guidelines for Human-AI Interaction (Amershi et al.) provide the practitioner-grade substance. The driving question: in your app, who decides what, when is the human in the loop, what does the AI explain, and how does trust get built or broken?

Teams map every decision point and handoff in their product — every place a user could be confused about whether they or the AI is making a choice — and take that straight back into the build.

To close, the instructor demonstrates a working AI agent they built, as inspiration for what's possible beyond a single-purpose app. Agents are deliberately out of scope for the graded project — most strong two-week products are simple apps that do one thing well — but a short, optional guide for building your own agent outside class is provided for those so inspired.

### Learning Objectives

- Apply design-thinking principles to human-AI interaction design.
- Use Amershi et al.'s guidelines as a structured audit tool for your app.
- Map every decision point and handoff in your product.
- Identify and design for the places where user trust is built or broken.
- Recognize where an agentic approach could help — and why most projects shouldn't reach for one.

### Required Readings

- Brown, T. (2008). *Design Thinking*. Harvard Business Review.
- Amershi, S., et al. (2019). *Guidelines for Human-AI Interaction*.

### Discussion / Cold-Call Questions

- In your app, what is the AI deciding without telling the user?
- Which guideline is your app violating most? Aligning with best?
- Where in your app would a user say “I have no idea what just happened”?
- What's a one-day test you could run to check a human-AI interaction assumption?
- Watching the agent demo: would an agent actually make your product better, or just more complicated?

### Assignments & Deliverables

- Build progress (team): apply your interaction map to the app; close the most confusing gap before the Build Day.
- Optional (individual): for the inspired, follow the take-home guide to build a simple agent outside class (ungraded). Optional reading: Schluntz & Zhang, “Building Effective Agents.”

## Day 8 — Responsible AI in Practice + Storytelling

**Wednesday, July 15 · 10:00 AM – 2:00 PM**

### Session Overview

Two anchor readings — Dignum and Floridi & Cowls — frame responsible AI as a design practice rather than a compliance exercise. We open with a short, structured case discussion drawn from the ISACA 2025 incidents brief, analyzed through these frameworks. Each team then pressure-tests its own product against them in discussion: where it might be biased, whose data it uses, what could go wrong, and who could be affected.

We close with storytelling for Demo Day and a dress rehearsal: each team gives a five-minute practice demo to a small peer group and gets structured feedback. How do you talk about what you built to people who didn't build it — a buyer, an investor, a hiring manager — and what makes a five-minute demo memorable?

### Learning Objectives

- Apply Dignum's “designing AI for human values” framework to your real product.
- Use Floridi & Cowls' five principles to evaluate your product's responsible-AI posture.
- Name the responsible-AI risks your product touches and how you'd speak to them.
- Shape and rehearse a clear, memorable five-minute Demo Day story.

### Required Readings

- Floridi, L., & Cowls, J. (2019). *A Unified Framework of Five Principles for AI in Society*.
- ISACA. (2025). *Avoiding AI Pitfalls in 2026*.

### Discussion / Cold-Call Questions

- Which of Floridi & Cowls' five principles is your product most at risk of touching?
- Whose data does your product use, and what would they think of that use?
- Who could be harmed if your product works exactly as intended? If it fails?
- In ten seconds: what does your product do, who is it for, and why does it matter?

### Assignments & Deliverables

- Final stretch (team): repo + deployed URL cutoff is 24 hours before Demo Day; use the rehearsal feedback to tighten both your build and your story.
